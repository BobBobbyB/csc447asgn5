How Do Study Time and Attendance Relate to Student Grades?

(0–20)
($0–$150k)

Design Brief

1. Dataset Choice

This project utilizes the “Factors Affecting University Student Grades” dataset from Kaggle (Atif Masih, 2023). The dataset contains over 10,000 student records, providing insights into how academic, social, and psychological factors influence performance. Key features include study hours, attendance, family income, and behavioral indicators such as motivation, stress, and self-esteem.

Source: Factors Affecting University Student Grades — Kaggle

2. Research Question

To what extent do study habits and attendance correlate with academic performance, and how do these relationships differ across gender and family income levels?

This project explores whether consistent attendance and increased study time predict stronger academic outcomes, while also examining how socioeconomic background and gender shape these results. By visualizing these variables together, the project highlights the interplay between individual effort and systemic inequality in education.

3. Visual Concept

A scatterplot visualization was selected to capture continuous relationships among multiple variables:

  • X-axis: Study Hours
  • Y-axis: Attendance (%)
  • Color: Grade (Red → Green)
  • Tooltip: Displays study hours, attendance, grade, gender, and family income
  • Filters: Gender and Family Income Range

This layout emphasizes clarity and multidimensional comparison, allowing users to explore how effort and opportunity intersect in academic achievement.

4. Interaction Concept

The visualization includes several key interactive elements to encourage exploration:

  • Hover Tooltips: Display individual student details, including attendance, grades, study hours, gender, and income.
  • Gender and Income Filters: Allow users to isolate subgroups for targeted comparison.
  • Study Hours Slider: Enables focused analysis of specific effort ranges.

These controls make the visualization both approachable and informative. Future enhancements could include regression trendlines, brushing, or zooming for deeper insight into dense clusters.

5. Design Inspirations

  • Ben Fry — Zipdecode: Demonstrates minimalist, intuitive data encoding.
  • Observable D3 Gallery: Provided reference for scatterplot interactions and transitions.
  • Namerology Baby Name Grapher: Inspired the use of interactive filtering and storytelling through data.

6. Write-Up

This visualization was designed to explore how study habits, attendance, and family income relate to student performance. I chose a scatterplot because it naturally represents relationships between two continuous variables (study hours and attendance). Each point corresponds to a student, allowing the viewer to see both overall patterns and individual variation.

Color encodes grades, ranging from red (low) to green (high), leveraging intuitive associations between hue and performance quality. Interactive filtering by gender, study-hour range, and family income was added to enable deeper exploration. A subtle hover zoom effect makes hovered points expand slightly, helping distinguish them in dense clusters. The double-filter system allows users to isolate specific groups to see how socioeconomic or behavioral factors affect academic outcomes. This design supports both overview and detail-on-demand interaction, following Shneiderman’s visualization mantra: “Overview first, zoom and filter, then details on demand.”

Initially, I considered encoding family income as color and grades as bubble size, but this approach made it harder to interpret performance patterns at a glance. I also experimented with a heatmap and small-multiples layout (e.g., comparing low- vs. high-income groups), but interactive sliders achieved similar clarity with less visual clutter. The final design prioritizes readability and exploratory flexibility.

The dataset was derived from a synthetic “Student Academic Factors” dataset frequently used in educational data-mining research and public repositories such as Kaggle. Data columns include Study_Hours, Attendance, Family_Income, Gender, and Previous_Grades. External references and inspiration used during development include:

  • D3.js Official Documentation
  • Observable D3 Examples (Scatterplot & Legend Patterns)
  • Shneiderman, B. (1996). The Eyes Have It: A Task by Data Type Taxonomy for Information Visualizations. IEEE Symposium on Visual Languages.
  • Color palette inspiration from ColorBrewer (Sequential Red-Yellow-Green Scheme)

Development Process:
The project was completed individually over several sessions (approximately 10–12 hours total):

  • Data Preparation & Exploration (~1 hour): Cleaned CSV data, mapped categorical grades to numeric values, and verified ranges.
  • Base Visualization Setup (~2 hours): Built the D3 scatterplot, axes, and responsive scaling system.
  • Interactivity & Filtering (~4 hours): Implemented dropdown and slider filters for gender, study hours, and income, along with dynamic axis rescaling.
  • Polish & Refinement (2–3 hours): Adjusted legend placement, tooltips, color encoding, and hover behavior for usability.

The most time-consuming aspect was debugging interactivity to ensure filters updated both data and axes smoothly. Tooltip behavior also required careful handling to prevent pop-ups from leaving the viewport. Dynamic rescaling after filtering was particularly important for maintaining visual clarity when focusing on smaller subsets of data.

This project demonstrates how interactive filtering and thoughtful visual encoding can reveal subtle relationships in data. Attendance values tended to cluster above 70%, limiting some variation, but overall the visualization effectively highlights complex patterns between study effort, income, and performance. In my interpretation, there appears to be no strong correlation between attendance or study hours and final grades, challenging assumptions about academic effort. The final result balances aesthetic simplicity with analytical depth, making it suitable for both educational research and classroom exploration.
